=== WASTE SEGREGATION GUIDE ===

Waste segregation is the process of dividing waste at the source into different categories to facilitate proper disposal, recycling, and composting. Proper segregation is the first and most important step in effective waste management.

Categories of Waste:
1. Wet Waste (Green Bin): Includes kitchen scraps like vegetable peels, fruit waste, leftover food, tea leaves, coffee grounds, eggshells, and garden waste like leaves and grass clippings. This type of waste is biodegradable and can be composted.

2. Dry Waste (Blue Bin): Includes paper, cardboard, plastic bottles, glass bottles, metal cans, newspapers, magazines, and packaging materials. These can be recycled and should be kept clean and dry.

3. Hazardous Waste (Red Bin): Includes batteries, e-waste like old phones and laptops, chemicals, paints, pesticides, medical waste, syringes, and expired medicines. These require special handling and should never be mixed with regular waste.

4. Sanitary Waste (Yellow Bin or Black Bag): Includes diapers, sanitary napkins, bandages, and contaminated cotton. These should be wrapped separately before disposal.

Tips for Segregation at Home:
- Place separate bins in your kitchen for wet and dry waste.
- Rinse plastic and glass containers before putting them in the dry waste bin.
- Never mix wet and dry waste as it makes recycling impossible.
- Store segregated waste in a dry, ventilated area to prevent odor.
- Teach all household members the segregation rules.

=== PLASTIC RECYCLING ===

Plastic recycling is a critical process that reduces plastic pollution and conserves natural resources. Plastics are categorized by resin identification codes (1-7) found on the bottom of containers.

Types of Recyclable Plastics:
- PET (Type 1): Water bottles, soft drink bottles, food containers. Widely accepted at recycling centers.
- HDPE (Type 2): Milk jugs, shampoo bottles, cleaning product containers. Highly recyclable.
- PVC (Type 3): Pipes, flooring, window frames. Rarely accepted; check with local recycling centers.
- LDPE (Type 4): Plastic bags, squeezable bottles, bread bags. Some grocery stores accept these.
- PP (Type 5): Yogurt containers, medicine bottles, bottle caps. Increasingly recyclable.
- PS (Type 6): Styrofoam cups, disposable cutlery, packing peanuts. Rarely recycled; try to avoid.
- Other (Type 7): Mixed plastics, large water cooler bottles. Difficult to recycle.

Steps to Recycle Plastic Properly:
1. Empty the container completely.
2. Rinse with water to remove food residue.
3. Remove caps separately (caps are often a different plastic type).
4. Flatten bottles and containers to save space.
5. Place in the designated dry waste or recycling bin.
6. Do not put plastic bags in regular recycling bins; take them to dedicated drop-off points.

=== COMPOSTING METHODS ===

Composting is the natural process of decomposing organic matter into a nutrient-rich material that improves soil health.

Method 1 - Backyard Composting: Create a pile or use a bin. Add alternating layers of brown materials (dry leaves, cardboard) and green materials (food scraps, grass clippings). Keep the pile moist and turn it every 1-2 weeks. Takes 2-6 months.

Method 2 - Vermicomposting: Uses red wigglers or earthworms to break down organic matter. Set up a bin with moist shredded paper bedding, add worms, and feed them fruit and vegetable scraps. Avoid meat, dairy, and oily foods. Great for apartments.

Method 3 - Bokashi Composting: An anaerobic fermentation process using Bokashi bran. Add food waste, sprinkle bran, seal the bucket. Can compost meat, dairy, and cooked foods. Ready in 2-4 weeks.

Method 4 - Tumbler Composting: A sealed rotating drum. Spin regularly; compost is ready in 4-6 weeks. Rodent-proof and odor-controlled.

What to Compost: Fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, tea bags, eggshells, grass clippings, dry leaves, shredded paper.
What NOT to Compost: Meat, fish, dairy, oily foods, diseased plants, pet waste.

=== MUNICIPAL WASTE RULES ===

Municipal waste management is governed by laws ensuring public health and environmental safety.

1. Mandatory Segregation: Most municipalities require citizens to segregate waste into wet, dry, and hazardous categories. Non-compliance can result in fines.
2. Collection Schedules: Wet waste may be collected daily; dry waste weekly or fortnightly. Put bins out before 7 AM.
3. Bulk Waste: Furniture and appliances require special arrangements. Contact your municipal corporation for bulk pickup.
4. Construction Debris: Must be disposed of separately. Never mix with household waste.
5. Penalties: Littering, illegal dumping, open burning, or failure to segregate can attract fines.

India Specific: Under Solid Waste Management Rules 2016, every household must segregate waste at source. Municipal apps and helplines are available for complaints.

=== MISSED GARBAGE PICKUP ===

If your garbage collection was missed:

1. Do Not Leave Waste Outside: Keep bins covered to avoid attracting animals.
2. Check the Schedule: Delays can occur due to holidays or vehicle issues.
3. Contact Local Municipality: Call your ward office or helpline. Mumbai: 1916, Pune: 020-25506800, Delhi: 1800-11-0093.
4. Use Municipal Apps: Apps like Swachh Bharat Urban or BBMP Sahaaya allow reporting missed pickups.
5. File a Complaint Online: Visit your city portal, file a complaint with address and date.
6. Contact Your Corporator: For recurring issues, escalate to your ward member.
7. Social Media: Many municipal corporations respond to complaints on Twitter or Facebook.

=== E-WASTE MANAGEMENT ===

E-waste includes discarded phones, computers, televisions, refrigerators, and batteries. Contains toxic materials like lead, mercury, and cadmium.

How to Dispose:
1. Manufacturer Take-Back Programs: Samsung, Apple, HP, Dell have take-back programs.
2. Authorized Recyclers: Drop off at Pollution Control Board-certified centers.
3. Retail Drop-offs: Many electronics stores accept old devices.
4. Never throw electronics in regular trash. Never burn e-waste.

=== ZERO WASTE LIFESTYLE ===

The 5 Rs of Zero Waste:
1. Refuse: Say no to things you don't need.
2. Reduce: Buy less, choose quality over quantity.
3. Reuse: Choose reusable bags, bottles, and containers.
4. Recycle: Properly segregate recyclables.
5. Rot: Compost your organic waste.

Tips: Carry reusable bags and water bottles. Buy in bulk. Repair electronics rather than replacing. Donate usable items instead of discarding.

=== APARTMENT WASTE MANAGEMENT ===

Best Practices for Housing Societies:
1. Install separate bins for wet, dry, and hazardous waste.
2. Set up a community composting unit for wet waste.
3. Partner with a licensed dry waste recycler.
4. Organize monthly e-waste collection drives.
5. Display visual signage in local languages on all bins.
6. Educate domestic workers and security staff on disposal procedures.
